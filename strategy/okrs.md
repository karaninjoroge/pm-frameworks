# OKRs — Objectives and Key Results

**Category:** Strategy / Goal-Setting
**Best For:** Aligning a team around ambitious, measurable goals for a quarter or year

---

## What It Is

OKRs are a goal-setting framework popularized by Intel and Google. They separate the qualitative ambition (Objective) from the quantitative measurement (Key Results).

- **Objective:** What are we trying to achieve? Qualitative, inspiring, clear.
- **Key Results:** How will we know we achieved it? Quantitative, specific, time-bound.

---

## The Key Principle

Key Results measure outcomes, not activities. "Launch the SMS registration feature" is an activity. "Reduce patient abandonment rate from 25% to 15%" is an outcome. The distinction matters because you can complete activities without achieving outcomes.

---

## Good vs. Bad OKRs

**Bad OKR:**
Objective: Improve the hospital queue experience
KR1: Launch SMS registration feature
KR2: Train 10 nurses on dashboard
KR3: Write 5 patient education materials

*Why it's bad:* All KRs are activities. Completing them does not prove the objective was achieved.

**Good OKR:**
Objective: Prove that information delivery reduces patient abandonment at pilot facility
KR1: Reduce abandonment rate from 25% to ≤15% by end of pilot
KR2: 70% of registered patients rate SMS updates as "useful" or "very useful"
KR3: Staff queue management time falls from 60 min/day to ≤30 min/day

*Why it's good:* Each KR is an outcome. You can achieve them only if the product is actually working.

---

## OKR Cadence

- **Annual OKRs:** Company direction. Set once a year.
- **Quarterly OKRs:** Team execution. Set at the start of each quarter.
- **Weekly check-ins:** Progress against KRs. Not new goals — just tracking.
