# MoSCoW Prioritization Framework

**Category:** Prioritization
**Best For:** Scoping a specific release with stakeholders when time and resources are constrained

---

## What It Is

MoSCoW divides features into four categories:

- **Must Have:** Non-negotiable. The product cannot launch without these.
- **Should Have:** Important but not launch-blocking. Build if time allows.
- **Could Have:** Nice-to-have. Deferred to next version if needed.
- **Won't Have (this time):** Explicitly out of scope for this release.

---

## When to Use It

- Scoping a specific sprint, release, or MVP with a team or stakeholders
- When you need a fast, shared decision about what is in and out
- When a product is at risk of scope creep and you need a forcing function
- Stakeholder alignment sessions where everyone needs to agree on priorities

---

## When NOT to Use It

- Comparing features across different user segments or effort levels — use RICE instead
- Early discovery when you do not yet know what to build at all
- When you need to communicate *why* something is prioritized — MoSCoW shows what, not why

---

## The Most Important Category

**"Won't Have (this time)"** is the most important category and the most neglected. Explicitly documenting what you will NOT build:
1. Prevents scope creep when stakeholders revisit decisions later
2. Signals that you have thought about the trade-offs, not just ignored ideas
3. Creates a backlog for future releases without losing good ideas

---

## Common Mistakes

**Everything is a Must Have:** If more than 40% of your features are Must Have, you have not actually prioritized. Must Have means the product is non-functional without it.

**No Won't Have list:** If you don't explicitly document what you are not building, it will be built.

**Using it without context:** MoSCoW does not explain *why* something is must-have. Always document the reasoning alongside the category.
