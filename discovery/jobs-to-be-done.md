# Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

**Category:** Discovery
**Best For:** Understanding the real motivation behind user behavior — beyond demographics and features

---

## What It Is

Jobs To Be Done is a theory of user motivation developed by Clayton Christensen. It reframes the question from "who is my user?" to "what is my user trying to accomplish?"

The core insight: people do not buy products. They hire products to do a job. When you understand the job, you understand what competing solutions exist, what success looks like, and what would make your product indispensable.

**Core format:** "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

---

## Why It Matters

Traditional personas define users by demographics: 34-year-old woman, small business owner, Nairobi. This tells you who she is but not why she does what she does.

JTBD focuses on the situation and motivation: "When I am sick and need to see a doctor, I want to know exactly when to arrive so I can preserve my working hours." This tells you what the product must accomplish — and reveals that the competition is not other queue management apps, it is the folk wisdom of "go on Tuesday" that patients currently use to manage uncertainty.

---

## Three Types of Jobs

**Functional:** The practical task ("I need to get medical care without losing a full day of income")
**Emotional:** How the user wants to feel ("I want to feel in control and not like I'm wasting time")
**Social:** How the user wants to be perceived ("I want to feel like I'm being treated with dignity, not as a number")

Great products address all three.

---

## How to Apply It

**Step 1 — Interview with JTBD in mind**
Ask about situations, not opinions. "Tell me about the last time you went to a public hospital" not "what do you want in a queue app?"

**Step 2 — Find the trigger**
What situation caused the user to seek a solution? What was happening in their life?

**Step 3 — Identify the job**
What were they trying to accomplish? Separate functional, emotional, and social dimensions.

**Step 4 — Map the competing solutions**
What did they do before your product existed? These are your real competitors.

**Step 5 — Define the hiring criteria**
What would make a user "hire" your product for this job? What does done look like?

---

## Example — Hospital Queue Management

**Old framing:** "Patients want shorter wait times"
**JTBD framing:** "When I need medical care on a working day, I want to know exactly when to be at the hospital so I can preserve my income and feel in control of my time."

**This changes everything:**
- The product is not competing with other queue apps — it is competing with uncertainty itself
- "Shorter wait" is not the job. "Predictable wait" is the job.
- A 3-hour wait with perfect information is better than a 1-hour wait with no information
